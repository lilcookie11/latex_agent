Due to source-target distribution changes, pretrained models often suffer severe performance degradation after deployment.
Continual test-time adaptation (CTTA) alleviates this issue by updating model parameters during inference with online unlabeled test data, making it important for real-world applications such as visual understanding and embedded intelligence \citep{wang2022continualtta}.
Recent studies have further extended CTTA to multimodal settings (MM-CTTA), such as audio-visual action recognition and event classification, and achieved strong performance by modeling modality reliability, selecting reliable modalities, performing asymmetric adaptation, or correcting supervision \citep{guo2025smoothing,yang2024read,chen2025tsa,he2026dasp}.
However, existing MM-CTTA methods still mainly rely on backpropagation, typically adapting model parameters through auxiliary objectives such as entropy minimization.
This creates a clear mismatch with practical edge-side deployment: edge devices usually provide limited computation and memory for online adaptation, and deployed models may run with inference engines or quantized components that do not support backward computation.
Therefore, this work studies edge-side MM-CTTA under resource-constrained, forward-only, single-sample streaming deployment, aiming to achieve stable and effective multimodal adaptation without backpropagation.

Existing backpropagation-free adaptation methods are mostly designed for single-modal models.
For example, BN-Adapt adapts models by updating BatchNorm statistics, while FOA relies on prompt updates in ViT architectures \citep{nado2020predictiontime,niu2024forwardonly}.
Their adaptation interfaces are usually tied to specific model components, making them difficult to directly transfer to MM-CTTA.
Zeroth-order optimization (ZOO) provides a more general forward-only learning mechanism.
It estimates update directions through forward perturbations and finite differences, requires no backpropagation, and does not rely on specific model structures.
However, directly applying ZOO to edge-side single-sample multimodal CTTA remains ineffective in practice.
Our diagnostic analysis identifies two main obstacles.
\textbf{First, local forward feedback and entropy fluctuations jointly cause direction drift.}
ZOO approximates update directions from perturbed objective differences, so its online optimization process naturally depends on the local feedback provided by the current test sample.
In single-sample multimodal streaming adaptation, modality imbalance and fusion bias can make such feedback unstable.
Meanwhile, under continual domain shifts, entropy-based losses can fluctuate noticeably, further amplifying the noise in direction estimation.
As a result, ZOO not only struggles to obtain stable update cues, but can also be pulled by instantaneous bias, causing the adaptation direction to drift over time.
\textbf{Second, high-dimensional multimodal parameter spaces reduce the search efficiency of ZOO.}
Multimodal models have larger parameter scales and more complex functional locations.
Directly perturbing the full parameter space not only slows the convergence of ZOO, but also disperses the limited forward-query budget over many parameters that are insensitive to the current domain shift.
This increases the variance of direction estimation, reduces the signal-to-noise ratio, and makes it difficult to quickly locate truly effective update directions.

To address the above challenges, we propose
\textbf{Z}eroth-\textbf{O}rder \textbf{S}election and \textbf{A}lignment \textbf{(ZOSA)}.
It performs zeroth-order updates by selecting reliable samples and key parameter layers, and stabilizes update directions through source-distribution alignment.
ZOSA consists of two key components, i.e., Reliable Sample and Layer Selection (RSLS), and Source-Anchored Objective Smoothing (SAOS).
Specifically, given edge-side single-sample streaming inputs, ZOSA first uses RSLS to select reliable samples and updatable parameter layers.
At the sample level, RSLS estimates class bias according to how frequently the current predicted class appears in a streaming history window, and combines this bias with prediction confidence to assess sample reliability.
Samples with lower prediction bias are identified as potentially reliable, while those with higher prediction bias are treated as potentially unreliable.
At the parameter level, RSLS uses a small amount of source-domain data to pre-select parameter layers that are more critical for adaptation, and uses them as the update space for ZOO.
By jointly selecting samples and parameter layers, RSLS concentrates the limited ZOO update budget on reliable samples and key parameter layers, thereby promoting learning from reliable samples, reducing dependence on unreliable sample feedback, and accelerating convergence in an effective parameter subspace.
Since entropy-driven objectives can still fluctuate sharply in unlabeled test streams with continual domain shifts, we further introduce SAOS to reduce this risk.
SAOS uses source-domain feature statistics as stable anchors and aggregates target-domain feature statistics through a lightweight streaming queue, constructing a source-target statistical alignment constraint.
This constraint provides smoother and more reliable optimization feedback than entropy alone, thereby stabilizing update directions under forward perturbations.

\begin{itemize}
    \item We propose ZOSA, a backpropagation-free framework for edge-side multimodal continual test-time adaptation. Built on zeroth-order optimization, ZOSA performs online adaptation using only forward passes under single-sample streaming deployment.
    \item We provide a diagnostic analysis identifying two core obstacles of applying ZOO to multimodal CTTA: direction drift caused by local forward feedback and entropy fluctuations, and inefficient search caused by high-dimensional multimodal parameter spaces. We address them with RSLS and SAOS, which stabilize ZOO from the search-space and objective perspectives, respectively.
    \item We conduct comprehensive experiments with CAV-MAE on Kinetics50-C and VGGSound-C multimodal corruption benchmarks, and further evaluate ZOSA on quantized backbones to validate its edge-side deployment value. Results show that ZOSA improves adaptation stability and overall performance while preserving backpropagation-free and low-resource adaptation.
\end{itemize}
