\section{Experiments}

\subsection{Experiments Setting}

\paragraph{Datasets.}
To comprehensively evaluate the effectiveness of our method for multimodal continual test-time adaptation, we conduct experiments on two widely used audio-visual corruption benchmarks, namely Kinetics50-C and VGGSound-C.
Following prior multimodal TTA settings, we apply 15 corruptions to the video modality and 6 corruptions to the audio modality, each with five severity levels.
Detailed construction procedures and protocol descriptions are provided in the appendix.

\paragraph{Baselines.}
We compare SZOA with representative CTTA methods, including Tent, SAR, READ, ContinualMAE, DeYO, and REM, as well as multimodal adaptation methods including PTA, BECoTTA-MoE, SuMi, and VIDA.
We further include backpropagation-free methods, FOA and T3A.
All baselines are evaluated under the same data streams and protocols, with detailed settings provided in the appendix.


\paragraph{Implementation details.}
For synthetic multimodal domain shifts, we use a CAV-MAE based pretrained audio-visual classifier and follow the standard setup of prior multimodal TTA works.
Unlike conventional batch-based online adaptation, we evaluate all methods in a single-sample streaming setting: test samples arrive sequentially, and the model adapts using only the current sample and a lightweight local history window.
We use ZOO as the online optimizer and update only the selected shift-sensitive layers in the modality branches, while keeping the fusion module frozen.
Unless otherwise specified, we set the streaming window length to $W=24$, select $K=3$ layers for each modality branch, use one ZOO step per test sample, and set the perturbation radius and learning rate to $\delta=10^{-3}$ and $\eta=10^{-2}$, respectively.
The alignment coefficient is set to $\lambda=0.4$.
All experiments are run on a single NVIDIA RTX 4090 GPU and repeated with five random seeds.
We report the average accuracy, with additional implementation details and hyperparameter settings provided in the appendix.


\subsection{Comparision with SOTAs}
We compare against a frozen \texttt{no\_adapt} model and a broad set of continual test-time adaptation baselines, including FOA, T3A, Tent, SAR, READ, ContinualMAE, DeYO, REM, PTA, BECoTTA-MoE, SuMi, and VIDA. Some methods have incomplete verified exports in a subset of co-shift settings. When only protocol-level summaries are verified, we keep the aggregate result in the cross-protocol summary table but omit unverified detailed rows from protocol-specific tables.

\paragraph{Robustness to synthetic multi-modal domain shifts.}
Tables~\ref{tab:k50_interleaved} and~\ref{tab:vgg_interleaved} report results on two interleaved single-modality corruption streams, where the audio and video modalities are corrupted alternately while the other modality remains clean, and the severity level is fixed at 5. Each entry denotes the classification accuracy at the corresponding local corruption position, while Avg.\ summarizes the average performance over the full stream. Under this single-sample streaming multimodal setting, existing CTTA methods provide only limited gains. In contrast, SZOA achieves the best average performance on both benchmarks, reaching $66.59\%$ on Kinetics50-C and $47.58\%$ on VGGSound-C. Compared with the frozen source backbone, SZOA yields relative improvements of $8.33\%$ and $14.54\%$, respectively. Compared with the strongest existing baseline on each dataset, it further improves by $3.88\%$ and $4.07\%$. These results show that our branch-restricted shift-sensitive layer selection and reliability-calibrated learning signal design effectively stabilize forward-only multimodal online adaptation under interleaved single-modality shifts.

\paragraph{Robustness to dynamic dual-modality domain shifts.} Tables~\ref{tab:k50_hard} and~\ref{tab:vgg_hard} report results under the PairTrack Async Hard protocol, where both audio and video are always corrupted and, within each fixed corruption pair, their severities vary asynchronously over time. The model must therefore remain stable under continuously changing cross-modal perturbation intensity. Existing methods degrade substantially under this protocol, whereas SZOA still achieves the best average performance, reaching $55.63\%$ on Kinetics50-C and $22.07\%$ on VGGSound-C. Compared with the frozen source backbone, this corresponds to relative improvements of $25.20\%$ and $75.16\%$, respectively. Compared with the strongest baseline FOA, SZOA further improves by $9.72\%$ on Kinetics50-C and $23.78\%$ on VGGSound-C. These results indicate that when multimodal shifts become stronger and more dynamic, our branch-restricted ZOO updates and reliability-calibrated learning signal can more effectively stabilize forward-only online adaptation.
